The 7/8 Approximation for MAX-3-CNF-SAT

CS155 · Class Notes
Randomized → Derandomized
§ 1 — The Problem

Satisfy as many 3-literal clauses as possible.

A 3-CNF formula is a conjunction (∧) of clauses, where each clause is a disjunction (∨) of three distinct literals. A literal is a variable like x₁ or its negation ¬x₁. 3-SAT asks whether all clauses can be satisfied simultaneously — that's NP-complete. MAX-3-SAT asks how many clauses we can satisfy in the best case — also NP-hard.

(x₁ ∨ x₂ ∨ ¬x₃) ∧ (¬x₁ ∨ x₃ ∨ x₄) ∧ (x₂ ∨ ¬x₄ ∨ x₅) ∧ (¬x₂ ∨ x₄ ∨ ¬x₆) ∧ (x₁ ∨ ¬x₅ ∨ x₆) ∧ …
The plan. Even though MAX-3-SAT is NP-hard, a trivial randomized algorithm — flip a fair coin for each variable — already satisfies at least 7/8 of the clauses in expectation. Then we'll derandomize it to get a deterministic polynomial-time 7/8-approximation. No cleverness, just probability.

§ 3 — Why P(satisfied) = 7/8 per Clause

Only one of eight assignments fails any single clause.

Take any 3-literal clause — say (x ∨ y ∨ ¬z). There are 2³ = 8 ways to assign (x, y, z). Step through them: only the assignment that makes all three literals false fails the clause. That's 1 of 8. So P(satisfied) = 7/8 for every 3-clause, no matter what literals appear in it.

It doesn't matter which literals. If the clause were (¬a ∨ b ∨ c) instead, the failing assignment would be a=T, b=F, c=F — different bits, but still exactly 1 of 8 outcomes. Every 3-clause has exactly one "bad" assignment under random coin flips. Conclusion: for any single 3-clause Cᵢ,  P(Cᵢ satisfied) = 7/8.
§ 4 — Linearity of Expectation → 7m/8

Sum the per-clause expectations. Independence not required.

Define Yᵢ = 1 if clause Cᵢ is satisfied, else 0. Then the total number of satisfied clauses is Y = Y₁ + Y₂ + … + Y_m. Each E[Yᵢ] = P(Cᵢ satisfied) = 7/8. By linearity of expectation:

E[Y]  =  E[Y₁] + E[Y₂] + … + E[Ym]  =  m · (7/8)  =  7m/8
The subtle bit. Clauses share variables, so the events "C₁ satisfied" and "C₂ satisfied" are not independent. But linearity of expectation doesn't care about independence — it always holds. That's why this proof is so clean: no probabilistic gymnastics, just arithmetic.
§ 5 — From Expectation to Existence

If the average is 7m/8, some assignment must hit ≥ 7m/8.

A random variable can't be strictly below its mean at every outcome — that would contradict the definition of mean. So among the 2ⁿ possible assignments, at least one satisfies ≥ ⌈7m/8⌉ clauses. This is the probabilistic method in its purest form: prove existence by averaging.

§ 6 — Derandomization · Method of Conditional Expectations

Process variables one at a time. Pick the side that preserves the average.

Here's the trick. The expectation E[Y | x₁, x₂, …, xₙ] over random assignments is 7m/8. After fixing x₁ = T, the conditional expectation might go up or down. But the average of the two conditional values E[Y | x₁=T] and E[Y | x₁=F] equals the unconditional E[Y]. So at least one of them is ≥ E[Y]. Pick that one. Repeat for x₂, x₃, …

Why is this polynomial? Computing each E[Y | partial] takes O(m): for each clause, count how many of its literals are already satisfied / unassigned. Repeat for n variables × 2 choices = O(nm). The result is a deterministic algorithm that provably outputs an assignment satisfying ≥ ⌈7m/8⌉ clauses. No randomness needed.
